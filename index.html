Goal

Modern architecture has been traditionally identified with the output of those designers participating in the CIAM conferences. In America, the scope was narrowed even further in Henry Russell-Hitchcock and Philip Johnson’s International Style exhibition at MoMA in 1932, which only included a narrow selection of mainly white structures, where volume prevailed over mass, balance replaced symmetry, and ornament was rejected. Many significant buildings of the time were left out and the later developments of modernism throughout the world were only briefly recollected in the architectural histories that were written after World War II.


This research aims to reveal the construction of these Eurocentric canonical visions on modern architecture by geographically mapping buildings mentioned in the traditional histories of architecture.


The result is a freely accessible, historiographical toolbox that can serve as the basis for a multitude of research on the subject. We hope that it can challenge the current historiographical narrative by emphasizing trans-national and trans-geographical approaches, providing a wider overview of architectural narratives of modernism in the world.


Method

In order to outline to what extent a Eurocentric perspective on modernism has distorted the recent history of architecture, this project maps the buildings mentioned, analyzed and included as an illustration in canonical histories of architecture dedicated specifically to modernism or those more global that include a part dedicated to modern architecture.


Using MapBox API to geolocate and visualize the data, this mapping helps identify what geographical areas and authors have been underrepresented in the traditional historiography and what issues have remained untackled in the architectural debate. 


Data>

In an initial stage, data has been gathered from the following books:

Charles Jencks, Modern Movements in Architecture (Harmondsworth: Penguin, 1973).

Spiro Kostof, A History of Architecture (New York: Oxford, 1985).

Fazio, Moffett, Wodehouse, A World History of Architecture (Enskede: TPB, 2006).

Ching, Jarzombek, Prakash, Global History of Architecture (New Jersey: Wiley, 2007).


Additional books have been analyzed and will be added in further iterations. These include titles before and after World War II, specific histories of modern architecture as well as global histories that contain parts dedicated to modern architecture.


Data from these books has been collected manually and was added into spreadsheets. The gathered data can be downloaded here. Beware that the data visualized on the webpage and the downloadable data might have discrepancies both in their content and structure. In some instances, data columns had to be separated or merged to map the data entries in MapBox. Beyond the information that is available online, the downloadable data set includes: coordinates, location names, built/unbuilt, chapter name, image caption, and count by book.



On Us


Mapping Histories is a research project by MIT Architecture and the Global Architectural History Teaching Collaborative (GAHTC), a project founded by Prof. Mark Jarzombek and Prof. Vikramaditya Prakash supported by the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation and managed by Eliana AbuHamdi Murchie. GAHTC has administrative connections to MIT's School of Architecture and Planning and its History, Theory and Criticism Program.Initial research was produced in 2021-2022 in conjunction with researchers from ETSAM, Madrid, and was supported by a MISTI Seed Fund Grant.


The MIT-based research team includes: Mark Jarzombek, Eliana AbuHamdi Murchie, Samuel DuBois, James Heard and Lasse Rau. The website was coded by James Heard and Lasse Rau.
